01Project Overview & Responsibility Matrix

The Green Leaf Lotus build is an 8-stall Tesla V4 Supercharger plus a 250 kW DC-coupled solar PV array, financed entirely by Future Tech 9000 on land leased from Green Leaf Partners. Total project cost: $1,391,500.

v2.0 · Tesla Audit Findings (May 2026)

What changed since v1.0: Audited every "Tesla provides / does not" claim against Tesla's official Supercharger for Business pages and public configurator (April 2026).

Hardware price reconciled: Tesla's configurator quotes $500,000 fixed for an 8-post V4 site (was estimated at ~$320K in early v1.0 drafts). Project budget already accounts for this.

Service capacity revised up: V4 Cabinet is rated at 1.2 MW shared across 8 posts, not 700 kW. NV Energy interconnection sized for 1500 kVA pad-mount transformer.

Tesla provides MORE than v1.0 credited: assigned project engineer who reviews drawings before permit submission, shipping & logistics, credit-card processing fees absorbed in the $0.10/kWh fee, Trip Planner + Google Maps integration, host pricing portal, preventative maintenance, ongoing remote diagnostics. Several were missed in v1.0.

Configurator-modeled site economics for Vegas (~320 kWh/post/day): ~$420K/yr revenue at $0.45/kWh retail, ~$208K/yr gross profit before fixed costs, ~5 year payback. Vegas falls between Texas and Florida on Tesla's published model.

This is the standalone operator model — Future Tech 9000 owns the equipment, holds the operator agreement with Tesla, and keeps the charging revenue (paying Tesla a per-kWh network fee). It is not the Tesla Host program where Tesla owns the asset and pays you a share of revenue. The standalone model is more work and more capital, but materially more revenue and full control.

02The Tesla Program — What We Bought

Tesla offers two business models for site hosts. Knowing which one we're in is foundational.

Tesla Host (the legacy model)

Tesla owns the equipment, designs the install, hires the contractors, pays the bills, keeps the revenue. The host gets a percentage of revenue or a flat lease payment. Pros: zero capital required from the host. Cons: no control over pricing, no upside in volume growth, Tesla can relocate the equipment with notice. Today this is rare and reserved for highest-tier corridor sites Tesla strategically wants. Not our model.

Supercharger for Business / Standalone (our model)

Future Tech 9000 buys the hardware from Tesla, pays for installation and infrastructure, owns the asset, and operates the station. Tesla provides the in-vehicle navigation routing, Trip Planner, Google Maps integration, the customer-facing app and billing, host-controlled pricing, payment processing (including credit card fees), the 97% uptime SLA, 24/7 driver support, and software updates. This is what the Green Leaf project is.

Tesla's published configurator12 as of April 2026 prices an 8-post V4 site at $500,000 hardware + $440,000 medium-tier installation = $940,000 all-in. Our project budget of $1,391,500 includes that $940K plus the 250 kW solar canopy ($125K), site contingency ($75K), and Future Tech 9000's deal reserves and soft costs. The hardware/install split is consistent with Tesla's transparency disclosures. Tesla's per-kWh network fee is $0.10/kWh of charging revenue.

Important

Tesla's standard Supercharger Agreement2 includes a self-operative SNDA clause favoring Tesla. In our case Future Tech 9000 is the equivalent of "Tesla" in that template — when we draft Green Leaf's lease, we mirror this protection: lender consent, non-disturbance, recordable memo. Without it, Green Leaf's bank could wipe out our $1.4M of equipment in a foreclosure.

What Tesla provides (per Tesla program docs11)

  • Approved plans & equipment specsStandardized site layouts, electrical drawings, equipment specifications for V4 hardware.
  • Project engineering support during installationA Tesla Design Engineer guides the customer through the install process. They review our contractor's work, sign off on key phases.
  • CommissioningTesla activates the cabinets after our electrical and NV Energy energization is complete. ~2 weeks per Tesla's published timeline.
  • Site listing in Tesla in-car navigation & Trip PlannerDrivers are automatically routed to our station based on state of charge, efficiency, site availability, and weather. Tesla's Trip Planner uses anonymous geofenced data to predict stall availability at arrival.
  • Google Maps integrationTesla and Google announced (March 2026) that Supercharger for Business sites appear in both Tesla apps AND Google Maps with forecasted stall availability and real-time wait times.
  • 3D Site MapsPilot feature. Shows our Supercharger layout, nearby businesses, and live availability to drivers.
  • Pricing portal & pricing toolsFuture Tech 9000 sets our own retail pricing through a dedicated host portal. Tools include congestion fees and time-of-use pricing to manage demand.
  • Payment processing — including credit card processing feesTesla collects from drivers and remits to us monthly. Credit card processing fees are covered by Tesla, not deducted from our share. This is real value we hadn't initially credited.
  • Credit card dispute handlingTesla manages chargebacks and select dispute resolution.
  • 97% uptime SLAIndustry-leading. Tesla operates and remotely monitors. Network Operations Center verifies escalations and disruptions 24/7/365.
  • Remote diagnostics, automatic triage, and preventative maintenanceTesla detects problems remotely and dispatches their own technicians (parts on hand, direct feedback to engineering) to repair.
  • 24/7 driver support — Tesla and non-Tesla EVsPhone support for all EV drivers using our station, not just Tesla owners.
  • Software updates and pricing model adjustmentsOTA firmware updates throughout the lease term. Battery preconditioning, AC ripple current heating to optimize charge speeds.
  • Replacement cabinets if equipment fails under warrantyStandard Tesla equipment warranty. Beyond warranty, parts at our cost.

What Tesla does NOT do

  • Pay for the chargersWe buy the cabinets, posts, and switchgear at Tesla's wholesale price.
  • Pay for installationTrenching, conduit, transformer pad, concrete, lighting, signage — all on us.
  • Pay the electric billNV Energy bills Future Tech 9000 directly for the meter that serves the station.
  • Hold the leaseWe are the tenant. Tesla has no privity with Green Leaf.
  • Pay property tax on the equipmentThe chargers are personal property of Future Tech 9000. We pay personal property tax to Clark County.
  • Site maintenance, snow, sweeping, lighting upkeepDay-to-day site presentation is on us, even though Tesla maintains the actual chargers.
  • Insurance on the equipmentTesla insures the cabinets in transit. Once installed, we insure them.

03Zoning & Land-Use (Clark County)

3463 Procyon St sits in unincorporated Clark County (Spring Valley township). Land-use jurisdiction is the Clark County Department of Comprehensive Planning. Permitting is the Clark County Department of Building & Fire Prevention.

Title 30 Unified Development Code

Clark County updated Title 30 in August 2023 to require new and renovated commercial and multifamily buildings to include EV-Capable conduit and dedicated electrical capacity3. The county also recognizes EV charging stations as a permitted accessory use in most commercial zones. Practical implication: our zoning fight is much easier than it would have been three years ago. We're not asking for a special exception; we're using land in a way the county has already declared a permitted accessory use.

What we still need to verify

  • Zoning of the parcel itselfPull the parcel record from Clark County Assessor and confirm the site is zoned C-2, H-1, or another zone where EV charging is a permitted accessory use. If the parcel is zoned R-3 or U-V (multifamily), we'll likely need a use permit hearing.
  • Setbacks from property linesTesla cabinet must sit outside required side/rear setbacks. Confirm with site survey.
  • ADA accessibilityAt least one of the 8 stalls must be ADA-accessible per Clark County code. Tesla's standard layouts include this.
  • Signage permitTesla branding and wayfinding signs require a separate signage permit. Limited to design Tesla approves.
  • Parking minimum recalculationIf the 8 stalls reduce the apartment complex's total parking count below code minimum, Green Leaf will need to evidence that surplus exists. Procyon site has surplus — verify.
Action item

Order a Title Report & Zoning Letter within Week 1. Clark County records office takes 5-7 business days. Cost ~$400. This is the single document that makes everything else conditional.

04Permits & Inspections

Clark County issues commercial permits at the Building & Fire Prevention Department, 4701 W. Russell Rd. Standard commercial review is 21 business days, which extends 10 business days for each correction cycle.4 Most projects go through 1-3 correction cycles.

Permits we need

  • Building PermitCovers the concrete pad for the equipment cabinet, foundations for the post stalls, any canopy structure for the solar PV. Plans submitted by a Nevada-licensed engineer.
  • Electrical PermitCovers the trenching, conduit, switchgear, transformer pad work, EV stall wiring. Electrical work by a Nevada-licensed C-2 electrical contractor.
  • Fire Department Plan ReviewEV charging stations require fire department review for fire suppression, vehicle clearances, and battery thermal-event response. Clark County FD reviews concurrent with building permit.
  • Signage PermitTesla wayfinding signage at site entrance and stall numbering. Separate permit, ~3 days.
  • Right-of-Way / Encroachment PermitIf trenching crosses public right-of-way (sidewalk, street) for utility connection, Public Works issues a separate ROW permit.
  • Solar PV Building PermitSeparate from the charging station permit. Clark County uses an expedited solar permit track for systems under 30 kW residential, but our 250 kW commercial system goes through standard review.

Inspections during construction

Schedule inspections through Clark County Citizen Access portal or by calling 702-455-30004. Each phase needs sign-off before progressing:

  1. Trench inspectionBefore backfill — verify conduit depth and bedding.
  2. Rough electricalAfter conduit is in but before final connections.
  3. Foundation / concrete inspectionFor equipment pad and post foundations.
  4. Final electrical inspectionBefore NV Energy energizes the meter.
  5. Final building / occupancyAfter Tesla commissioning is complete and the site can operate.
Reality check

Clark County permits in 2026 are running long. Plan for 35-45 calendar days from submission to issuance, not 21 business days. Use an expediter (e.g., Permit Place, Building Engineering Group) — they cost $1,500-$3,000 but typically save 2-3 weeks of correction cycles.

05Power: NV Energy Interconnection

Eight V4 stalls, each capable of 250-325 kW, need a 700-1,000 kW service. That's a primary-voltage service requiring a new dedicated transformer. NV Energy's process for this is governed by Rule No. 9 (Line Extensions) and Schedule EVCCR-TOU (the EV-specific commercial tariff)5.

Tariff structure: Schedule EVCCR-TOU

EVCCR-TOU is NV Energy's special rate for DC fast charging. Three things to know:

  • Demand charge reductionEVCCR-TOU reduces the standard $/kW demand charge — replaced with a smaller volumetric ($/kWh) adder. This is critical because demand charges otherwise dominate operating costs at low utilization.
  • 10-year transition periodThe reduced rate is in effect from April 2019 through April 2029. After 2029 we transition to whatever NV Energy's then-current EV rate is.
  • 500-meter cap statewideEVCCR-TOU is limited to 500 installed meters across all of NV Energy's service territory, first-come first-served. The cap is being approached. Apply for the meter as early as possible — losing the rate would add ~$30-50K/yr in demand charges.

Time-of-Use periods

EV electricity rates vary by time of day. Off-peak overnight pricing (10 PM - 8 AM) is dramatically cheaper than mid-day peak (June through September, 1 PM - 7 PM). The economic implication: solar offset matters most during summer afternoons, exactly when our 250 kW PV array generates most of its output. Designed integration.

Interconnection process

  1. Submit Service ApplicationThrough NV Energy's Builder Services portal. Includes load calculation showing 700-1,000 kW peak draw, single-line diagram, site plan.
  2. NV Energy load studyEngineering reviews whether the existing distribution feeder can support the new load. If not, an upgrade to the upstream feeder may be required (paid by NV Energy if existing customers benefit, paid by us if dedicated to our project).
  3. Cost estimate & line extension agreementNV Energy issues a binding cost estimate. Customer-side costs (transformer pad, primary conduit on our property, switchgear) are ours; utility-side (the transformer itself, primary cable from the pole) is theirs but billed back if dedicated.
  4. Pay deposit & sign agreementTypically 50% of the line extension cost up front.
  5. ConstructionNV Energy schedules transformer delivery and primary cable. This is the long lead — 8-16 weeks for transformer delivery in 2026. Order the transformer before lease signing if possible.
  6. EnergizationAfter Clark County final electrical inspection passes, NV Energy energizes the meter. Tesla commissioning starts after energization.
Critical path

The transformer is the longest lead time of the entire project. Order it Week 1, not Week 10. If we wait until permits are issued, we add 8-16 weeks to the back end. Pay the deposit even before lease signing if Green Leaf agrees in writing — it's refundable if the deal collapses.

NV Energy DCFC Rebate Program

NV Energy offers commercial EV charging rebates: 50% of project cost up to $400/kW or $40,000 per station, whichever is less, with a maximum of 5 stations per project.6 Per-station means per stall in our case. Eight stalls × $40,000 cap × 5 station maximum = $200,000 rebate ceiling. The remaining 3 stalls are not rebate-eligible. Apply through NV Energy's Custom Grant Program for a possible second-tier additional grant for projects beyond the standard rebate scope.

06Solar PV: Engineering & Interconnection

The 250 kW solar PV array is sized to offset roughly 20-25% of annual station electricity, generates the 30% Solar ITC, and provides daytime peak-shaving (drives down our peak demand bill).

System architecture

  • 250 kW DC capacityRoughly 600-650 panels at 400-450W each, depending on selection. Mounted on a parking-canopy structure over the chargers (best Vegas insolation, also provides shade for waiting drivers — UX win).
  • InvertersEither string inverters (cheaper) or microinverters (per-panel monitoring, easier maintenance). For a commercial canopy at this scale, central string inverters are typical.
  • DC-coupled or AC-coupled to charger?For our setup, AC-coupled is standard: solar inverts to 480V AC, ties into the same switchgear feeding the chargers. Direct DC-coupling to the charger is technically possible but unusual.
  • Battery storage (optional)Adding 100-200 kWh of battery storage would shift solar output to evening peaks and could earn an NV Energy demand-response payment. Costs another $80-150K. Not in current budget. Worth modeling separately.

NV Energy Solar Interconnection

NV Energy's net-metering successor program now uses 15-minute interval netting in the northern territory and is moving in that direction in southern Nevada7. Practical implication: solar exported to the grid only offsets consumption within the same 15-minute window. This is less generous than monthly netting, and it's why pairing solar with battery storage is increasingly the right answer.

For our project, the simpler path is to not export to the grid at all — design the system as behind-the-meter, sized so PV output is fully consumed by the chargers during operating hours. This avoids net-metering paperwork entirely and the system functions as a load-reducer rather than a generator.

Solar permits and inspections

  • Solar building permit (Clark County)Filed with the rest of the package. Includes structural calculations for the canopy, electrical single-line diagram, equipment cut sheets.
  • Solar electrical inspectionBefore tying into the main switchgear. Same inspector schedule as charging station electrical.
  • NV Energy meter swap or new meterFor behind-the-meter solar, no meter change needed. For grid-export, NV Energy installs a bidirectional meter.
  • Production ActivationOnce electrical passes and meter is set, the system is energized and starts producing.

07Tax Credits & Depreciation

This is where the largest portion of project economics live. Total Year 1 federal tax benefit: ~$425,000 (depreciation deduction at 37% bracket + ITC). Get this wrong and the deal economics fall apart.

The Big Three (in order of value to us)

1. 100% Bonus Depreciation — ~$387,000 Y1 tax savings at 37%

Under OBBBA (Pub. L. 119-21, July 2025), 100% bonus depreciation is permanently restored for qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 20258. The Tesla cabinets, posts, switchgear, and solar equipment all qualify. Annual cost recovery property class: 5-year for the EV chargers (MACRS class 57.0), 7-year for site improvements, 5-year for the solar PV. Since bonus is 100%, full deduction in Year 1.

Installation (civil, electrical, transformer pad)Tesla "Medium" tier estimate: $55K/post × 8. 15-yr property, 100% bonus.$440,000
Solar PV (basis-reduced after ITC)5-yr property, 100% bonus$106,250
Total Y1 deduction$1,046,250
Tax savings at 37% bracket$387,113

2. Solar ITC (Section 48E) — $37,500

30% of qualified solar PV cost ($125,000 × 30% = $37,500). Critical good news for our project: the 250 kW system is well under the 1 MW threshold. Per IRS guidance, solar projects under 1 MW are exempt from prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements and automatically qualify for the full 30% credit9. We capture the full ITC without prevailing-wage paperwork, complications, or audit risk.

Per IRS §50(c), the solar property basis is reduced by 50% of the credit ($18,750), making the depreciable basis $106,250 instead of $125,000. Bonus depreciation applies to the reduced basis.

Win

The "under 1 MW = no prevailing wage" exemption saves us roughly $30,000 in compliance costs and meaningfully reduces audit exposure on the ITC. Do not exceed 1 MW on the solar array. If we ever consider expanding to 500 kW or more, recheck this threshold.

3. §30C Charger Credit — EXCLUDED

The Alternative Fuel Vehicle Refueling Property Credit (§30C) provides up to 30% credit on EV charging equipment. The Inflation Reduction Act extended it through 2032, but OBBBA sunsets §30C effective June 30, 2026. Our 16-week build, starting after lease signing in late 2026, cannot capture this credit. We've excluded $46,500 of potential credit value. If we accelerate the lease and break ground before June 30, 2026, we recapture this — worth pursuing aggressively.

Construction-start deadline cliff

Hard deadline

OBBBA sunsets the Section 48E Solar ITC for new construction starting after July 4, 2026, AND for any project not placed in service by December 31, 202710. To preserve the 30% ITC, we must begin construction (physical work of significant nature, OR 5% safe-harbor of total cost incurred) before July 4, 2026, and place the system in service before December 31, 2027. Both deadlines are achievable on our timeline but only with disciplined execution.

08Prevailing Wage / Apprenticeship

Most discussion of solar ITC prevailing wage and apprenticeship (PWA) requirements describes the rules for projects 1 MW and larger. For the Green Leaf project these rules do not apply because our 250 kW array is below the 1 MW threshold9. We claim the full 30% ITC without PWA documentation.

What we do anyway

Even though not required, document construction labor hours and contractor classifications. Two reasons:

  • Audit defenseIf the IRS ever challenges the ITC claim, having clean labor records pre-empts the conversation. Cheap insurance.
  • Possible SBA wage reportingSBA 504 financing has Davis-Bacon implications when federal money is involved. The 504 program technically does not require Davis-Bacon (it does for 7(a) loans for federal construction), but documenting wages keeps options open.

If we ever expand to ≥1 MW

Future-proof: if the apartment community ever wants additional solar (say, rooftop solar across the apartment buildings) and total project capacity crosses 1 MW, PWA kicks in. Davis-Bacon prevailing wages from sam.gov apply for 5 years post-construction. Apprentices must perform 15% of construction hours. Good-faith effort exception available if no apprentices respond to RFPs.

09SBA Financing & NEPA Trigger

Future Tech 9000 finances the project through a SBA 504 loan. SBA 504 is a long-term, fixed-rate loan for fixed assets (real estate and major equipment), typically 50% conventional bank lender + 40% SBA-debenture-funded CDC + 10% borrower equity.

NEPA implications

Federal financial assistance can trigger National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) review. SBA 504 financing on real property typically requires either a Categorical Exclusion (CatEx) or an Environmental Assessment (EA). For Phase I-clean, urban-infill, paved-parking-lot improvements, a CatEx is the routine outcome.

Phase I Environmental Site Assessment

Required as part of SBA 504 underwriting. ASTM E1527-21 standard. ~$3,500-$5,500. Looks for historical contamination, USTs, vapor encroachment. If clean, deal proceeds. If recognized environmental conditions (REC), Phase II soil and groundwater testing may be required ($10K-$50K). For a parking lot with no industrial history, we expect Phase I clean.

Other SBA 504 conditions

  • Personal guaranteeOwners with 20%+ stake in Future Tech 9000 personally guarantee the SBA portion.
  • Job creation requirementSBA 504 typically requires creation of one job per $90K of SBA debenture (loosely enforced for green energy projects).
  • Minimum borrower equity 10%For new businesses or special-use property, can rise to 15-20%.
  • Owner occupancy not requiredTenant operator is fine for our scenario.
  • 20-year termMatches our lease primary term. Critical alignment.

10Insurance Stack

Insurance carriers in 2026 increasingly view EV charging stations as a fire-risk loaded asset. Battery thermal events get headlines; carriers price the risk. Plan for ~$15K/year in premiums, not the $8K-$15K range cited in older industry guides.

Conditions to negotiate with carrier

  • A-rated carrier minimumAM Best A- or better. Not because Green Leaf demands it (they do) but because second-tier carriers exit the EV market in any softening.
  • Green Leaf as additional insured on CGLStandard. They get notice of any cancellation.
  • 30-day notice of cancellationStandard.
  • Annual certificate of insurance to Green LeafProcess item, not just at lease signing.
  • Per-occurrence vs annual aggregateGet $2M per occurrence specifically. Some carriers try to consolidate to a single $4M aggregate that's effectively half-coverage.

What Green Leaf must do (in their lease obligations)

Coordination item

Green Leaf must notify their property carrier of the EV charging station before construction begins, not at next renewal. Some property insurance carriers will treat unannounced EV chargers as a material change of risk and decline a future claim. Their annual renewal will likely have a 5-15% premium loading for the station.
