01Money & Rent

Where 80% of the meeting will live. The landlord wants more money; we want predictability.

Q1.1"Why only $2,000? My parking is worth more than that."
$2K/month is the guaranteed floor — paid no matter what, with zero business risk on the landlord. Add the 3% annual escalator and they collect $275,133 over the 10-year guaranteed term — about $35K more than flat rent. To match this risk-adjusted income from a different tenant, they'd need a credit-rated commercial lessee on a similar-length lease, which is rare for parking-lot space.
Data: Projected gross site revenue at full ramp ≈ $329K/yr ($0.45/kWh Vegas blended × 250 kWh/stall/day × 8 stalls × 365). The $24K Y1 rent is ~7% of projected gross — comparable to industry ground-lease ratios for fueling sites. For context: 8 stalls + equipment pads occupy roughly 1,750–3,000 sqft of parking lot, and parking-lot ground space leases at a fraction of premium retail rates (Vegas retail averages $1–5/sqft/month per Cushman & Wakefield Las Vegas MarketBeat) because it lacks the frontage and visibility that drive retail pricing.
If pushed: "We're absorbing 100% of operating risk. If charging volume drops, demand charges spike, or Tesla changes rates — your $2K never moves. We could swap to a higher % revshare instead, but you'd lose the floor entirely. The market data is clear: guaranteed cash with an escalator is worth more than variable upside on a single-use asset."
Q1.2"What if inflation runs 6–8%? I'm stuck at 3%?"
The 3% compound escalator is calibrated to the Fed's long-run inflation target and tracks the historical CPI 20-year rolling average. Over 20 years, the escalator delivers $165K more than a flat rent — even if inflation averages 2%, you still come out far ahead. If they prefer, we can substitute a CPI tie-in with a floor and cap.
Data: BLS CPI-U 20-year rolling averages have stayed at 2.3–3.0% in every period since 1990 except the brief 2021–2023 spike. Historical 30-year average: 2.5% (BLS CPI-U historical series). A 3% fixed escalator outperforms CPI in 18 of the last 30 years.
DECIDE: Will we offer CPI tie-in (2% floor / 5% cap) if pushed?
Q1.3"What if Future Tech 9000 can't pay rent? You vanish?"
Three protections: (1) Future Tech is investor-funded, not loan-funded — no debt service we could default on. (2) Personal guarantee on the first [12–24] months of base rent. (3) Six-month security deposit held in escrow or letter of credit. Combined, the landlord has a multi-layered safety net that doesn't depend on Future Tech's operational success.
Data: Standard tenant-funded charging deals include personal guarantees of 12–24 months of base rent plus a 3–6 month security deposit. Commercial real estate rent receivership rate for performing tenants exceeds 98% industry-wide (IREM industry benchmarks).
DECIDE: Personal guarantee depth (12 mo, 24 mo, or "good guy" structure)
Q1.4"Why no profit share? If this prints money, why don't I see any?"
A profit share would mean they also share the losses. Pure guaranteed rent isolates them from revenue volatility — DC fast-charging revenue varies 3× or more between high-traffic and slow months, between seasons, and between competitive cycles. We chose the structure landlords ask for when they understand the volatility: predictable cash with an escalator.
Data: Tesla Supercharger network reached 75,000 stalls and delivered 6.7 TWh in 2025, but per-stall quarterly revenue varies widely by location, season, and route competition (Tesla Charging blog, March 2026). EV charging utilization at new sites typically takes 18–36 months to reach steady-state.
If pushed: "If you want upside exposure, we can add a 5% kicker above a defined profit threshold — but the trade is $2K base + variable kicker vs. $2K base + 3% escalator. Most sophisticated landlords pick the escalator."

03Operations & Tenant Impact

These are the questions about how the chargers physically affect the property and its existing tenants.

Q3.1"How many of my parking spaces do I lose?"
Initial estimate: 8–10 spaces. We're building 8 stalls total — one of which will be ADA-compliant if code requires it (we don't add a 9th stall for ADA, it's one of the 8). Beyond the stalls themselves, the cabinet pad and transformer pad add a small footprint, typically placed at the lot edge or corner to minimize parking loss. Final count is locked during the walk-through (Step 1 of the process).
Data: 8 parking stalls ≈ 1,300 sqft (9×18 ft each). Cabinet pad ~150–300 sqft, transformer pad ~150 sqft, ADA-required clear floor area built into the existing stall footprint (not an extra space). Total site footprint typically 1,750–3,000 sqft depending on circulation needs and existing lot geometry.
Q3.2"Will my restaurant tenant be upset? Will they break their lease?"
Most restaurant tenants see EV charging as a customer acquisition asset, not a threat. We'd recommend including them in the walk-through. EV drivers spend an average 23 minutes on-site charging — that's a captive food-buying audience your restaurant tenant doesn't get from regular street traffic.
Data: Average DC fast-charging dwell time: 23 minutes (Convenience & Energy Advisors / Convenience Store News, 2024). 32% of EV owners factor "proximity to food/restaurants" into where they charge; 61% are more likely to shop at retailers with charging (Numerator EV Owner Survey, April 2024, n=4,000+). Real transaction data — Sheetz Breezewood PA: $9 on charging, $10.76 on food at the same visit (Bloomberg, July 2022). Tesla Hollywood Diner Q4 2025: $1M+ in restaurant revenue, 920 fries orders/day driven by charging traffic (Tesla Q4 2025 disclosed data).
Q3.3"Are the chargers loud? My apartment tenants will complain."
The charger cabinets themselves run 50–70 dB during operation — between a quiet conversation and a vacuum cleaner. The louder noise actually comes from the cars being charged: their own battery-cooling fans can hit 80+ dB on hot summer days. Mitigation: place cabinets 30+ feet from residential windows, add a 3–4 foot landscaping hedge (cuts ~5 dB), and use the existing parking-lot acoustic profile to absorb the rest.
Data: DC fast charger cabinet noise: 50–70 dB at 1m (industry analysis, 2025). For reference: refrigerator hum 50 dB, normal conversation 60 dB, vacuum cleaner 70 dB (CDC NIOSH noise reference). Vehicle battery-cooling fans during fast charging are widely reported at 75–85 dB but vary by car and weather (Tesla owner forums, multiple sources).
If pushed: "Vegas already runs heat-related fan and AC noise across the property all summer. The marginal noise from chargers is small relative to the existing acoustic floor. We can also commit to a noise study during the walk-through and adjust placement based on findings."
Q3.4"What about lighting at 2 AM? My residents will hate it."
Tesla V4 sites use dark-sky compliant LED, fully down-cast, dimmable. We can zone-disable any specific fixtures that affect residential windows after 10 PM. The lighting is also motion-activated at low ambient activity, so it's not full brightness when nobody's charging.
Data: Tesla V4 lighting specs meet Clark County Title 30 dark-sky ordinance. Typical canopy lumens 30–50 per sqft, dimmable to 10% off-peak. Comparable to commercial parking-lot standards already on the property.
Q3.5"Drivers will sit for 30 minutes. Will they use my restaurant's bathrooms?"
Some will. Three ways to handle it: (a) Treat as a foot-traffic benefit — driver dwell time → food + bathroom + retail spend at your restaurant tenant. (b) Install a portable restroom on Future Tech's dime (~$200/month all-in). (c) Build a permanent restroom; if paired with a small mart, could itself be revenue-generating.

04Legal & Lease

Their lawyer will ask these. Don't wing them.

Q4.1"Why does my lender need to sign an SNDA?"
SNDA = Subordination, Non-Disturbance & Attornment. Without it, if their mortgage ever defaulted or they refinanced with a hostile lender, the new owner could kill our lease in foreclosure — and we'd lose $1.4M of equipment. With it, the lease survives ownership changes. It also protects them: if the lease survives, their rent stream survives too.
Data: SNDA is industry-standard for any commercial lease where the tenant is making material capital investments. Required by all institutional lenders and most regional banks.
Q4.2"What if my lender refuses to sign the SNDA?"
Backup paths: (a) We negotiate directly with the lender on alternate language — most banks will sign SNDAs because they prefer rent-paying tenants in foreclosure scenarios. (b) If the lender refuses outright, the deal doesn't close — we'd absorb the pre-construction costs and walk. We can't put $1.4M of equipment at risk without lien protection.
Data: SNDA refusal in commercial real estate is rare (industry estimates <5% of cases). Usually indicates stressed lender or unusual loan covenants.
Q4.3"End of lease — who removes the equipment? Who repaves?"
Future Tech, at our cost. Lease specifies: complete equipment removal, asphalt restoration to existing standards, electrical decommissioning, and final site survey before turnover. Triggered by lease expiration or earlier mutual termination. We post a removal/restoration bond at lease signing so the landlord has financial assurance even if Future Tech vanishes.
Data: Industry-standard ground-lease practice — landowner gets land back to original condition. Restoration bond typically 5–10% of original installation cost.
Q4.4"Can you assign or sublease to someone else? A competitor?"
Assignment allowed only with landlord consent (not unreasonably withheld), or to affiliated entities. Sublease prohibited. Tesla's own operator agreement requires Future Tech to remain the named operator of record.
Data: Standard commercial ground-lease assignment language. Tesla Supercharger for Business program prohibits operator substitution without Tesla approval.
Q4.5"What if I sell the property in Year 7?"
The lease runs with the land. Any buyer takes the property subject to the lease — including the SNDA, which protects them from being forced to terminate. Many sophisticated buyers see a stable long-term tenant with an escalator as a value-add, not a burden.
Data: Standard commercial real estate practice. A short-form lease summary recorded at Clark County provides public notice to all potential buyers — they can't claim ignorance of the lease terms.

05Construction & Disruption

How long, how loud, how much does it interrupt their tenants' lives.

Q5.1"How long is construction? How much disruption to my tenants?"
Two phases. Pre-construction (permits, lender approvals, equipment order, NV Energy transformer): typically 3–5 months. Active construction: typically 4–8 weeks. Work happens daytime hours only (7 AM–5 PM), no weekend work without notice, all tenant access maintained throughout. We can't guarantee specific timelines — NV Energy transformer lead times can stretch the schedule.
Data: NV Energy commercial transformer lead times in 2026 range 14–26 weeks based on regional demand. Active construction window for 8-stall V4 + 250 kW solar is typically 4–8 weeks once the transformer is on-site.
Q5.2"Will my power be cut during install?"
Possibly 1–2 short outages (4–8 hours each) during NV Energy transformer cutover. All outages scheduled with property management 30+ days in advance, performed during off-peak hours (typically 6 AM–10 AM weekdays), and announced to tenants ahead of time.
Data: Standard NV Energy interconnection practice for new commercial service. Some installations achieve cutover with zero customer-side outage via temporary feed; depends on site electrical layout.
Q5.3"What if your contractor damages my building?"
All contractors carry $5M liability insurance, performance bonds, and explicit restoration guarantees. Future Tech indemnifies Green Leaf against any contractor-caused damage. Repair triggered within 48 hours of any reported incident.
Data: Standard pre-construction insurance package for commercial site work. Specifications confirmed before any contractor mobilizes equipment.

06Risk & "What If"

The scenario questions a careful landlord (or their lawyer) will run through.

Q6.1"What if Tesla cancels your host agreement?"
Their rent is guaranteed by the lease, not by Tesla. If Tesla terminated (extremely rare — would require us to materially breach Tesla's terms), we'd convert the site to non-Tesla CCS DC fast charging and keep operating. The hardware can be substituted; the lease keeps paying regardless.
Data: Tesla Supercharger network reached 75,000 stalls and 6.7 TWh delivered in 2025. Host site termination rate is industry-anecdotally negligible — Tesla's incentive is to keep functioning sites open (Tesla Charging blog, March 2026).
Q6.2"Charger fire, driver injury, parking-lot incident — who's liable?"
Future Tech is liable for any incident arising from charger operation. We carry $5M general liability + $5M umbrella, with Green Leaf named as additional insured on all policies. Cross-indemnification means: their existing property-side issues are covered by their insurance; charger-related issues are covered by ours.
Data: Standard insurance structure for tenant-funded EV infrastructure on leased land. Cross-indemnification language is industry-standard in CRE.

07Tax & End-of-Lease

The CPA's questions — and the ones a landlord raises when they're thinking 10 years ahead.

Q7.1"Will my property tax assessment go up?"
Charging equipment is filed as Future Tech's personal property in Nevada — separate from the real estate. Should not trigger reassessment of the land or building. We recommend confirming with the Clark County Assessor before lease execution to lock the treatment in writing.
Data: Nevada Revised Statutes §361.030 — trade fixtures owned by the tenant are taxed as personal property to the tenant, not as part of the underlying real estate. Standard treatment confirmed via assessor letter in most commercial deals.
Q7.2"Can I terminate early if I want to redevelop or sell?"
Two structures available: (a) Hard no during the 10-year guaranteed term — keeps the deal clean and financeable. (b) Landlord termination allowed at Year 10+ with 24-month notice and a termination fee equal to 36 months of base rent. Future Tech retains right-of-first-refusal on any redevelopment plan.
DECIDE: Hard-no termination or termination-fee structure? Sophisticated landlords will ask.
Q7.3"At Year 10 renewal, can I reset rent to market rate?"
Recommended structure: 3% escalator continues uninterrupted into both renewal periods — consistent with the "predictable income" pitch. Alternative if they push hard: at Year 10 renewal, both parties commission a fair-market-rent appraisal; new rent set at the greater of (a) escalated rate from Year 10 or (b) 90% of fair-market-rent. This caps their downside and gives them upside if the market has shifted.
DECIDE: 3% continues (simple, landlord-friendly) vs FMR floor structure (more complex, more upside)
